Selling internationally? Here is everything you need to know about invoicing across borders — from currency handling to VAT compliance.

Cross-border e-commerce is projected to reach $7.9 trillion by 2026, according to Juniper Research. If you sell products or services to international clients, your invoicing process needs to handle complexities that domestic sellers never face: multiple currencies, varying tax regimes, and compliance requirements that differ from country to country.

This guide covers the essential knowledge every international seller needs to invoice correctly and avoid costly mistakes.

Currency and Exchange Rate Considerations

When invoicing internationally, the first decision is which currency to use. You have three options:

  • Invoice in your local currency. This is simplest for your bookkeeping, but the client bears the exchange rate risk. Some clients may resist this, especially if the rate fluctuates significantly.
  • Invoice in the client's currency. This is more client-friendly and reduces disputes. However, you absorb the exchange rate risk, and the amount you receive may differ from what you expected when you issued the invoice.
  • Invoice in a major reserve currency (USD, EUR). A common compromise for international trade. Both parties share the exchange rate risk equally.

Best practice: Always state the invoice currency clearly, include the currency code (USD, EUR, GBP, CNY, JPY), and note the exchange rate if you are converting from another currency. Tools like InvoiceCraft support 7 major currencies out of the box.

Tax Implications: VAT, GST, and Sales Tax

Tax treatment of cross-border sales varies dramatically depending on what you sell and where your customer is located.

For digital products and services:

  • EU (VAT): Since 2015, digital services sold to EU consumers are taxed at the customer's local VAT rate. You may need to register for VAT or use the One-Stop Shop (OSS) system to report and remit VAT across all 27 EU member states.
  • UK (VAT): Post-Brexit, the UK has its own VAT regime. Digital services to UK consumers require VAT registration if sales exceed the threshold (currently GBP 85,000/year, but zero-threshold for non-UK businesses).
  • Australia (GST): Digital products sold to Australian consumers are subject to 10% GST. Non-resident suppliers must register if their Australian turnover exceeds AUD 75,000.
  • Canada (GST/HST): Foreign digital service providers must register and collect GST/HST on sales to Canadian consumers.

For physical goods: Import duties, customs declarations, and local sales taxes apply. The invoice must include accurate product descriptions, HS codes, and declared values for customs clearance.

What Your International Invoice Must Include

A compliant cross-border invoice typically requires more information than a domestic one:

  1. Your business legal name, address, and tax ID (VAT number, EIN, or equivalent).
  2. Client's full legal name and address. For B2B transactions in the EU, the client's VAT number is required on the invoice.
  3. Invoice date and unique invoice number. Sequential numbering is especially important for tax audits.
  4. Description of goods or services. Be specific enough for customs purposes if shipping physical goods.
  5. Unit prices and quantities in the invoiced currency.
  6. Tax breakdown. Show VAT/GST as a separate line item with the applicable rate. For zero-rated exports, explicitly state "Zero-rated export" or "Reverse charge mechanism."
  7. Incoterms (for goods). Terms like FOB, CIF, or DDP clarify who bears shipping costs and risk.
  8. Payment terms and bank details. Include SWIFT/BIC codes and IBAN for international wire transfers.
